### Basic Physics
#### Electric Charges
- Two types of charges: positive charge (by protons), and negative charge (electrons)
	- opposite charges attract, like charges repel
- A moving stream of electric charge is electric current
- Accelerating charges generate electric and magnetic fields
	- [Electromagnetism](Electromagnetism.md) - Maxwell's Equations
- Electronics control and amplify the flow of electric current
- Coulomb discovered "like charges repel, opposite attracts"
- Unit of charge (Q or q) is coulomb (C), an electron carries $-1.6\times10^{-19} C$
- Movement of electric charge is called an electric current
- Flows through conductors, but not through insulators
- Unit is coulomb/second (C/s) or amperes (A)
- electrical current I can be represented as the rate at which charge flows $I = dQ/dt$ where Q is the electric charge transferred over a time t
- Electric fields are caused by electric charges, as described as the Coulomb force per unit charge $E = F/q$
- Electric potential (Voltage) at a point is energy required to bring a unit charge from infinity
	- Measured in **volts** 
	- The higher the electric potential the more energy
- When electric charges move, they create magnetic fields
- Electromagnets - a coil of wires becomes a magnet when electric current runs through it
- A time-varying magnetic field can create an electric field, and a time-varying electric can e a magnetic field
	- Synchronised oscillations of electric and magnetic fields are electromagnetic waves

## Electrical circuit
An electric la circuit is an interconnection of electrical components such that an electric charge can flow along a closed path.
Electrical current ($i$): time rate of flow of electrical charge, unit is amperes ($A$) equivalent to coulombs per second.
Voltage ($V$): the force motivating charges to "flow" in a circuit is called voltage.

In an alternating current (AC) circuit, current changes direction periodically, such as a sine wave
$V(t) = V_psin(2\pi ft + \phi)$ 
Normal AC is three phase. Nikola Tesla invented AC.

In a direct current (DC) circuit, current only flows in one direction. Can convert AC to DC using a rectifier. Thomas Edison invented DC.

Current tends to move through conductors with some degree of friction. This opposite motion is resistance. 
**Ohm's Law**: Electrical current through a conductor between two pints is directly proportional to the voltage across the two points;
- Constant of proportionality, the resistance, measured in ohms ($\Omega$)
- $V = IR$
- $I=V/R$
- $R=V/I$

### Kirchhoff's Current Law
For any node in an electrical circuit, sum of all currents flowing into a node equals the sum of currents flowing out
$\sum_{k=1}^{n} I_k = 0$

### Kirchhoff's Voltage Law
The directed sum of voltages around any closed loop is zero
If the circuit has multiple loops, KVL is true for every loop
$\sum_{n} v_n = 0$

### Analog to Digital Conversion (ADC)
Most signals are analog (continuous in time) in nature
Computers only handle discrete data, usually in the binary form of 1,0
![[Pasted image 20250317170419.png|300]]![[Pasted image 20250317170244.png|300]]

Analog data needs to be digitised to be stored, processed or transmitted between computers. This involves processes such as sampling, quantisation, and encoding
![[Pasted image 20250317170615.png]]
A continuous analog signal is transformed into a discrete digital signal by sampling. Sampling involves a switch operating at a frequency.
![[Pasted image 20250317170819.png]]
**Nyquist Theorem**: when sampling rate is twice the highest frequency of original signal, the signal can be uniquely represented and retrieved back.
- **Aliasing** is a type of distortion and occurs when the sampling rate falls below Nyquist Rate
- $F_s >= 2F_{max}$

##### Example
CDs have a sample rate of 44.1[kHz](Hertz.md), which means 1 second of music has 44100 samples taken

#### Quantisation
**Quantisation** is the process of mapping values form a continuous or large set to a smaller, discrete set, often used to reduce precision or memory usage.
![[Pasted image 20250317171841.png]]
It attempts to match sampled values with the closest value in a limited # of discrete levels:
- 3-bit quantiser: $2^3 = 8$ levels
![[Pasted image 20250317172009.png|300]]
##### Quantisation Error
Quantisation error is the discrepancy between actual sampled value and quantised value
##### Quantiser's Resolution
The quantiser's resolution is measured in bits, and determines the number of quantisation levels
- $N$-bit quantiser: $2^N$ quantisation levels

## Power Delivery
We step up and step down the voltage and current of electricity from a plant before commercial use. As usually this energy has to travel long distances, we step down the current and step up the voltage to keep power ($P$) constant in according to:
- $P = IV$ 
We do it this way as $P_{loss}$ is calculated by:
- $P_{loss} = I^2\cdot R$  
hence, the lower the current, the lower the power loss when transporting electricity. Power loss still occurs, as all wires have some resistance and power is lost as **heat**.
